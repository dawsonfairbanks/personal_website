Research

I study soil microbial response to climate change including changes in temperature, precipitation, snowpack, and fire disturbance. Soils hold the largest carbon pool in Earth's terrestrial sphere and understanding how microbes will feedback to either accelerate or reduce climate change impacts is the core of my research. I use a combination of machine learning, remote sensing, metagenomics, and physiological assays to answer critical questions in microbial ecology and ecosystem science.

Seasonality and Snowpack Influence on Microbial Functional Traits

Microbes cycle nutrients in soil and are subject to changes in environmental conditions and climates. In high elevation forests, snowmelt acts as an environmental filter on microbial communities leading to large scale microbial die-off and elevated nitrogen and nutrient cycling. This research examines the influence of snowpack dictating the trade-off of microbial functional traits using a metagenomic traits to determine community level allocation of growth, resource acquisition and stress tolerance traits as a function of seasonality. I am funded for this work as the Principal Investigator of a Department of Energy CSP New Investigator award as part of a short one-year postdoctoral research work in collaboration with the CZNET GeoMicro cluster

Undergraduate Research: Testing models of central carbon metabolism for soil microbial communities

I began working for Dr. Paul Dijkstra in the Ecosystems Climate Change and Solutions group during the summer of 2012 at Northern Arizona University. My project investigated soil carbon (C) cycling using mathematical models to predict microbial biochemical processes that contribute to soil C storage and release. Soils hold the largest C pool in terrestrial ecosystems and poor understanding of physiological processes in situ hinders predictions of feedbacks with changing environmental conditions and climate. My experiment tested the accuracy of these metabolic models used to calculate biochemical processes in intact soil microbial communities using stable isotope probing techniques.
