Research

I am interested in microbial biogeochemistry and global change. My PhD dissertation research at the University of Arizona examined microbial responses to fire disturbance and snow melt in high elevation forests as part of the Critical Zone Observatory Network. I use a combination of physiological and meta-omic approaches to answer cutting edge questions in microbial ecology. I am also interested in data science synthesis efforts and have participated in large-scale integration efforts with the National Ecological Observatory Network (NEON), Long Term Ecological Observatory Network (LTER) and CZO. I apply my data science and machine learning skills to integrate observations from multiple sources (soil, water, gas, molecular) to better understand how microbes respond to environmental change.

Outreach

I am passionate about bringing science to a wider audience. Throughout my career, I have been active in volunteer opportunities involving STEM education, curriculum development, public outreach events, social media communication and environmental restoration projects.

Resources

I have worked hard to develop my data science skills and have created resources to share these skills and to train students I mentor. I am a regular instructor as part of the CyVerse Data Carpentry Workshop series and have compiled a list of resources for scientists to incorporate FAIR data principles into their workflows. I believe that science should be reproducible and creating and annotating workflows is integral to the work that I do.
